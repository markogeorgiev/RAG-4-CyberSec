Social engineering attacks try to manipulate you into giving up information, access, or money. Here's how to spot them:

**Common red flags**

Urgency and pressure — "Act now or your account will be closed." Manufactured time pressure is meant to stop you from thinking critically.

Authority impersonation — Someone claiming to be IT, your bank, the CEO, or a government agency, often requesting something unusual.

Requests for sensitive info — Legitimate organizations rarely ask for passwords, full card numbers, OTP codes, or 2FA codes via call/email/text.

Too good to be true — Unexpected prizes, refunds, inheritances, or investment returns.

Unusual payment methods — Gift cards, wire transfers, or crypto are favorites because they're hard to reverse.

Mismatched details — Sender addresses that are slightly off (paypa1.com), hovering over links reveals a different destination, generic greetings instead of your name.

Emotional manipulation — Fear, curiosity, sympathy ("I'm stranded and need help"), or flattery.

**Tactics to recognize by name**

Phishing (mass deceptive emails), spear phishing (targeted at you specifically), vishing (phone), smishing (SMS), pretexting (an invented scenario to build trust), baiting (a tempting lure like a "found" USB drive), and tailgating (following someone through a secure door).

**How to verify**

Independently contact the organization using a number or address you find yourself, not one provided in the message. Don't click links — navigate to the site directly. Slow down; urgency is the enemy of good judgment. When in doubt, confirm through a second channel (call a colleague who supposedly emailed you).

**General defenses**

Use multi-factor authentication, keep software updated, limit what you share publicly (attackers research targets), and trust your gut — if something feels off, it probably is.

The core principle: any unsolicited contact asking you to act quickly, share secrets, or move money deserves skepticism and independent verification.
