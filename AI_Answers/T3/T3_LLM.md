Securing your home Wi-Fi comes down to a handful of key steps:

Use strong encryption. Set your router to WPA3 if available, or WPA2 (AES) at minimum. Avoid WEP and WPA, which are outdated and easily cracked.

Change default credentials. Replace the router's default admin username and password (the login for the router's settings, not the Wi-Fi password). Use a long, unique passphrase for the Wi-Fi itself—12+ characters.

Update firmware. Check for router firmware updates regularly, or enable auto-updates if supported. These patch security vulnerabilities.

Disable risky features. Turn off WPS (the push-button pairing feature) and remote management/admin access from outside your network unless you specifically need it. Disabling UPnP can also help, though it may break some devices.

Set up a guest network. Keep visitors and smart-home/IoT devices on a separate guest SSID so they can't reach your main devices.

Rename your SSID. Change the network name from the default (which can reveal your router model) to something that doesn't identify you personally.

A few extras worth doing: position the router centrally to limit signal bleed outside your home, reboot it occasionally, and review the list of connected devices now and then to spot anything unfamiliar.

The biggest wins are strong WPA2/WPA3 encryption, a long Wi-Fi passphrase, a changed admin password, and current firmware. Those four cover most of the risk.
